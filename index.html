Definition

What is climate action?
Climate action refers to any measures taken to reduce or mitigate the negative impacts of climate change. This can include reducing greenhouse gas emissions, adapting to changing climate conditions and promoting sustainable development practices. Climate action is important because climate change has the potential to cause significant harm to the planet and its inhabitants, including more frequent and severe natural disasters, rising sea levels, and food and water shortages. By taking action to address climate change, we can help to reduce these risks and create a more sustainable future for ourselves and future generations.

What is the primary reason behind climate change?
The primary driver of current climate change is the rapid increase in greenhouse gas concentrations in the atmosphere, mainly carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), nitrous oxide (N2O), and fluorinated gases. These greenhouse gases trap heat from the sun in the Earth's atmosphere, creating a "greenhouse effect." As their levels rise, the Earth's average temperature increases, leading to global warming.
Human activities significantly contribute to greenhouse gas emissions. The burning of fossil fuels (coal, oil, and natural gas) for energy, deforestation, industrial processes, and agriculture are major sources of greenhouse gas emissions. Land-use changes and certain industrial activities also play a role.

What are the consequences of climate change?
Rising temperatures:Global average temperatures have been increasing, leading to heatwaves and extreme weather events.
Melting ice and rising sea levels:Glaciers and polar ice caps are melting, contributing to rising sea levels, which pose risks to coastal communities and ecosystems.
Changes in precipitation patterns:Some regions may experience more intense rainfall and flooding, while others may face prolonged droughts.
Ocean acidification:Increased CO2 levels in the atmosphere also lead to higher CO2 absorption in oceans, causing ocean acidification and harming marine life, particularly coral reefs and shell-forming organisms.
Impact on ecosystems and biodiversity:Climate change disrupts ecosystems, affecting plant and animal species and leading to shifts in their habitats.
Economic and social impacts:Climate change can impact agriculture, water resources, health, and livelihoods, exacerbating existing vulnerabilities in vulnerable communities.

What is SDG-13?

SDG-13 refers to the thirteenth of the United Nations' Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), which are focused on "Climate Action." The goal is to take urgent action to combat climate change and its impacts, by reducing greenhouse gas emissions, building resilience to climate-related hazards, and promoting sustainable development practices.

What is climate change?
Climate change refers to long-term shifts in global or regional climate patterns, primarily due to human activities, leading to alterations in Earth's climate system. These changes involve variations in temperature (SDG 13 - thay đổi nhiệt độ), precipitation, wind patterns, and other weather-related elements.

What is El Niño?
- El Niño is a phenomenon that occurs when warm water on the surface of the Pacific Ocean moves from east to west, resulting in an increase in sea surface temperature. This affects weather patterns and climate globally.
- El Niño typically lasts for several months and occurs irregularly, usually every 2 to 7 years.
- The impacts of El Niño include heavy rainfall, floods, intense tropical storms and hurricanes in certain regions, along with droughts and wildfires in other areas.

What is La Niña?
-La Niña is the opposite phenomenon of El Niño, occurring when cold water on the surface of the Pacific Ocean moves from west to east, resulting in a decrease in sea surface temperature.
-La Niña also lasts for a prolonged period and occurs irregularly, usually every 2 to 7 years.
-The impacts of La Niña typically include droughts in some regions, floods in other areas, and an increased likelihood of tropical storm development.
-El Niño and La Niña are important factors in forecasting and understanding climate change and their influence on weather systems and the environment on Earth.

What is carbon credits?
Carbon credits are a unit of measurement for carbon emissions, used to quantify the amount of emissions that a business, organization, or country saves, reduces, or eliminates from their production processes or activities.
Carbon credits are typically calculated in metric tons of CO2 equivalent, where one metric ton of CO2 equivalent corresponds to the amount of CO2 emissions released when burning one metric ton of coal or an equivalent amount of fossil fuel.
Carbon credits can be bought, sold, or traded on the carbon market to support emission reduction activities or to achieve the carbon reduction goals of a country or organization.

What is green credits?
-Green certificates are a unit of measurement and certification for activities or products that are sustainable and have minimal negative impact on the environment.
-Green certificates can be applied to sustainable products, projects, or activities in various fields, including renewable energy, environmental protection, resource management, sustainable agriculture, and climate change mitigation.
-Green certificates are typically certified by specialized organizations or programs, ensuring that the recognized activities or products are trustworthy and genuinely beneficial for the environment and society.

The Amazon Butterfly Effect
-The Amazon Butterfly Effect, also known as the "butterfly effect in the climate system," is a concept in the field of weather and climate science. It originates from a principle in the theory of nonlinear dynamics, sometimes referred to as the butterfly effect or domino effect.
-This concept is derived from the question: "If a butterfly flaps its wings in the Amazon, can it cause a hurricane in China?" While humorous in nature, it emphasizes that a small and seemingly insignificant change in one system can lead to significant and unpredictable impacts in another complex system.
-In the context of weather and climate, the Butterfly Effect in the Amazon suggests that a small change in conditions in one area can have a large influence on the climate and weather in distant regions. For example, deforestation in the Amazon rainforest leading to a reduction in the area of pristine forests can decrease the CO2 absorption capacity of the forest, increase the remaining CO2 in the atmosphere, and impact global climate.

Net zero
-"Net zero" refers to the state in which the amount of a particular substance, usually greenhouse gases, emitted into the atmosphere is equal to the amount removed from the atmosphere. The term is often used in the context of addressing climate change, particularly in relation to carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions.
-In the context of achieving "net zero carbon emissions" or "net zero greenhouse gas emissions," it means that the total amount of CO2 or other greenhouse gases released into the atmosphere from human activities (such as burning fossil fuels, deforestation, and industrial processes) is balanced by an equivalent amount removed from the atmosphere or offset through various means.
-Achieving net zero emissions is crucial in the fight against climate change because it helps stabilize the concentration of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere, preventing further warming of the planet. It involves reducing emissions through various strategies, such as transitioning to renewable energy sources, improving energy efficiency, and adopting sustainable practices in various sectors. Additionally, offsetting can be done through actions like afforestation (planting trees to absorb CO2) or investing in carbon capture and storage technologies.

TRANSPORTATION

THE PROBLEMS WITH TRANSPORTATION

The smoke emitted from motorbikes in Vietnam poses several significant challenges and environmental issues. As motorbikes are the primary mode of transportation in the country, their exhaust emissions contribute significantly to air pollution. But other than motorbikes, railways, cars, and buses are also vehicles that cause air pollution. The smoke contains harmful pollutants such as carbon monoxide, hydrocarbons, and particulate matter, which have adverse effects on public health and the environment. High levels of air pollution can lead to respiratory problems, cardiovascular diseases, and other health issues, especially in densely populated urban areas.

Moreover, the increasing number of motorbikes on the roads has exacerbated the problem of traffic congestion. As traffic slows down or comes to a standstill, the motorbike engines continue to emit smoke, adding to the overall air pollution levels. This creates a vicious cycle, where traffic congestion worsens air pollution, and vice versa. The combination of traffic-related emissions and smoke from motorbikes contributes to poor air quality and reduced visibility on the roads, making it challenging for both commuters and pedestrians.

STATISTIC

Based on Forbes Vietnam's chart, the carbon dioxide emissions per kilometre travelled are approximately 103g for motorbikes, 105g/km for buses, and 192 g/km for midsize gasoline cars. Road transport is the primary contributor, responsible for 85 percent of total emissions, followed by internal waterway transport at 8 percent (remaining constant from 2020-2030), airways at 5 percent, and sea transport at 2 percent. Rail transport generates the least emissions.

According to ClimateWatch, Vietnam's CO2 equivalent emissions in 2020 totaled 458.14 million tonnes, accounting for 0.88% of global emissions. The Ministry of Transport's Environment Department predicts that carbon dioxide emissions from the transportation sector will reach an estimation of 65 million tons by 2025 and 89 million tons by 2030.

IMPACTS

Pollution
The exhaust emissions from motorbikes release harmful pollutants such as carbon monoxide, nitrogen oxides, and particulate matter, contributing to air pollution. This degrades air quality and harms the natural environment.

Climate Change
Motorbike emissions, including greenhouse gases like carbon dioxide, contribute to global warming and climate change, affecting weather patterns and leading to more frequent extreme weather events.

Health Care Costs
The health impacts of motorbike emissions result in increased medical expenses, putting a strain on healthcare systems and affecting individuals' ability to work and contribute to the economy.

Respiratory and Cardiovascular Issues
The pollutants in motorbike smoke can cause respiratory problems, such as asthma and bronchitis, and exacerbate cardiovascular diseases, leading to increased health care costs and reduced productivity.

Premature Deaths
Exposure to air pollution from motorbikes is associated with premature deaths due to respiratory and cardiovascular diseases, impacting the overall public health in Vietnam.

Reduced Labor Productivity
Poor air quality from motorbike smoke can lead to sick days and reduced work efficiency, affecting the overall productivity of the workforce.

SOLUTION

01. Raise awareness

Raising awareness and educating the public about the environmental impact of fossil fuel-powered vehicles is essential to promote eco-friendly transportation choices and inspire collective efforts to reduce air pollution, greenhouse gas emissions, and their detrimental effects on the planet.

02. Vehicles

A complete shift to green transport alternatives by 2050 is pivotal including marine and river transport. Establishing widespread charging infrastructure to accommodate the growing demand for sustainable energy in transportation is crucial. Encouraging Vietnamese ships to align with Annex VI of the MARPOL Convention and the IMO's emissions reduction strategy is vital. Restricting and discontinuing the manufacturing and import of fossil fuel-dependent vehicles is important.

03. Upgrading infrastructure

One of the solutions is to devise a strategy for replacing outdated railway vehicles with electric and green energy-powered trains, aiming towards a sustainable and low-emission transportation future. This initiative involves upgrading the existing railway infrastructure, constructing new electrified lines, and transitioning away from coal dependence in train operations to utilizing green energy.

VIETNAM’S EFFORTS & FOREIGN COUNTRIES ASSISTANCE

Vietnam is taking various measures to improve the issue of emissions from motorcycles. Some of the measures include implementing new emission standards for motorcycles, promoting the use of electric motorcycles and cleaner vehicles, supporting the development of public transportation infrastructure to reduce traffic congestion, and raising awareness about the impact of motorcycle emissions on the environment and human health.

Afforestation projects led by organizations like the "Vietnam Administration of Forestry," play a crucial role in combating erosion and promoting sustainable ecosystems. VARS and the Project Management Board of Forest Protection and Development Investment in Dakrong District initiated the Forest Planting and Restoration Project at the headwaters of the Thach Han River in 2022. Following this, VinFast launched the program "For a Green Future for Everyone" intending to plant one million trees. For every VinFast electric vehicle sold in Vietnam, the company will contribute a tree to green projects and reforestation at the headwaters. Alongside VARS and VinFast, Future U - an environmental advocacy organization - introduced the idea of "Biove - a two-way social network connecting people with the mission of living green" to support citizens in contributing financially to tree planting initiatives named after themselves.

Vietnam actively collaborates with foreign nations to address the threats of floods, droughts, and landslides. Notably, Japan has supported Vietnam's disaster preparedness through Official Development Assistance programs, aiding in the construction of resilient infrastructure such as riverbanks and embankments. Australia's partnership focuses on water resource management and sustainable agriculture, sharing expertise in efficient irrigation techniques. Additionally, Vietnam engages with regional organizations like ASEAN and its Disaster Emergency Response Fund to ensure coordinated disaster responses. Through these international partnerships, Vietnam enhances its capacity to effectively mitigate the impact of floods, droughts, and landslides. Currently, Germany and the local community are working together through the Mekong Climate Resilience Program to cultivate climate-resistant crops. They are employing innovative techniques such as alternate wetting and drying to cultivate rice using up to 30% less water! These solutions are paving the way for a more climate-resilient Southeast Asia.

CATASTROPHE

PROBLEMS
LANDSLIDE
Landslides in Vietnam, including those at Bao Loc Pass, Dak Nong, and Sóc Sơn, are primarily caused by climate change impacts and inadequate land management practices. Factors such as erratic rainfall, deforestation, and improper construction contribute to weakened soil structure and increased landslide risks. Regions like Sapa, Lao Cai City, Bao Thang, Bao Yen, and Van Ban are particularly susceptible to flash floods and landslides.

Reservoir in Dak Nong at risk

FLOOD
Floods in Vietnam are caused by climate change, heavy rainfall, river overflow, urbanization, and deforestation altering river flow. The recurring weather pattern of El Niño in the Pacific Ocean exacerbates the situation, leading to higher temperatures, intense heatwaves, changed precipitation patterns, saltwater intrusion, and intensified storms in Central Vietnam. Provinces and cities like Quang Binh, Quang Tri, and Thua Thien Hue are particularly vulnerable to severe flood impacts due to their geographical location and local weather conditions.

Flood ravages district in central Vietnam

DROUGHT
Drought in Vietnam is caused by various factors and affects different regions. The occurrence of El Niño disrupts atmospheric circulation patterns and ocean temperatures, leading to reduced rainfall in areas like the North Central Coast, highlands, and the South Central Region. Vietnam's complex terrain, with mountains and narrow valleys, contributes to uneven rainfall distribution. Unsustainable water usage, resource exploitation, deforestation, infrastructure development, and rising sea levels from North Pole ice melting exacerbate drought conditions.

Northern crops devastated by drought

IMPACTS

Vietnam's agriculture and economy are suffering devastating consequences from climate change. Droughts, floods, and landslides have decimated agricultural productivity, leading to food scarcity and significant economic losses. Communities are frequently uprooted and face disrupted access to healthcare and education due to flooded infrastructure. Tragically, increased drowning incidents, water shortages, malnutrition, and loss of life are widespread. In 2020 alone, 132 deaths, 207 injuries from floods and landslides, and over 2.7 million people affected by droughts underline the crisis's magnitude. Urgent and comprehensive measures are needed to mitigate risks, protect lives, and safeguard livelihoods.

Globally, climate change greatly affects agriculture, and Vietnam, a major rice producer, is combating saline intrusion and rising sea levels that caused a 5% decline in rice production since 1961. The northern and central highland regions face prolonged droughts and intense heat. Crop and livestock diseases further harm farmers. Solutions include efficient irrigation, agricultural mechanization, crop diversification, and accurate weather forecasting to address these challenges. The Asian rice market, heavily influenced by climate change and phenomena like El Niño, experiences fluctuations in supply due to excessive rainfall or droughts in different countries, leading to rising rice prices that are forecasted to continue increasing in the future.

SOLUTION

Landslide

Land-use planning
Implement proper land-use planning regulations to prevent construction in flood-prone areas. This includes avoiding building on floodplains, maintaining natural drainage systems, and preserving wetlands.

Infrastructure development
Construct and maintain effective drainage systems, including canals, ditches, and stormwater management facilities. This helps divert excess water away from populated areas and reduces the risk of flooding.

Emergency preparedness
Educate communities about flood risks and develop emergency response plans. This includes training emergency personnel, establishing evacuation routes, and stockpiling necessary supplies.

International cooperation
Foster collaboration and information sharing between countries and regions prone to flooding. This can involve sharing environmental practices, coordinating response efforts, and providing assistance during emergencies.

Flood

Implementing a system to drain water into reservoirs
This involves constructing reservoirs to collect rainwater and floodwater, which helps in controlling and directing the flow of water through drainage channels.

Building protective embankments and dams
Constructing embankments and dams around rivers, canals, or flood-prone areas to prevent the spread of water and protect the surrounding communities.

Flood-resistant houses
Utilizing flood-resistant house designs such as floating houses, container houses, or houses supported by concrete pillars. These designs are specifically engineered to withstand water and flooding, ensuring the safety of residents during flood events.

Constructing shelters with sufficient water and food supply for one month
Establishing shelters equipped with an adequate water supply and food stockpiles to ensure the survival and well-being of affected individuals during prolonged flood situations.

Planting water-retaining forests
Implementing afforestation programs to create forested areas that can serve as natural barriers against floodwaters. These forests help absorb and retain water, reducing the velocity and force of the floodwater.

Drought

Constructing water reservoirs for water storage
Store water during periods of abundant rainfall, which can then be utilized during dry spells or drought conditions.

Researching and developing water desalination techniques
Investing in research for efficient seawater desalination creates freshwater sources in coastal areas with limited resources.

Constructing artificial canals for drought rehabilitation
Building artificial canals to redirect water from water-rich areas to regions experiencing drought, ensuring a more equitable distribution of water resources.

Planting mangrove forests
Implementing afforestation programs to establish mangrove forests, which act as natural barriers against saltwater intrusion, protecting agricultural lands and communities in the Mekong Delta region.

Harvesting rainwater
Implementing rainwater harvesting systems to capture and store rainwater for various uses, such as irrigation and non-potable water needs.

Drought-resistant crops
Using techniques like hydroponics will promote climate resilience and reduces water demand in Southeast Asia.

THE PROBLEMS WITH ENERGY

Vietnam's rapid economic development has led to surging demand for energy, creating immense pressure to meet these needs without augmenting negative environmental impacts. Presently, Vietnam still heavily relies on fossil fuels contributing significantly to greenhouse gas emissions and climate change.

The lack of a proper electricity pricing mechanism has hindered agreements between investors and EVN (Vietnam Electricity), preventing completed wind and solar power plants from connecting to the grid. Dependence on fossil fuels, insufficient investment and infrastructure, climate variability, and the lack of adequate renewable energy sources have contributed to the instability of Vietnam's electricity supply.

Sustainable energy resources like solar and hydropower also have their drawbacks. Hydropower produces 2,214,000 MW of electricity globally, with Vietnam contributing to 1.4% of production. However, large reservoirs cause environmental issues, including land loss and increased salinity. It also emits greenhouse gasses, affecting forests and biodiversity. Solar panels have a 35-40 year lifespan, but take years to decompose, posing energy and environmental challenges worldwide.

IMPACTS

1. Contribute to greenhouse gas emission

Reliance on fossil fuels to produce energy emits greenhouse gases like CO2, SO2 and NOx. A 1,000 MW coal plant releases 5 million tons of CO2 and 18,000 tons of NOx annually, along with toxic waste. Oil drilling causes pollution in air, water, and soil. Burning oil and gas also produce harmful gases, posing further environmental concerns.

2. High demand, low supply

Recently, the Northern region has faced electricity shortages around 4,9000 MW due to hot weather, low water levels in reservoirs, and the impact of El Nino. Rising coal prices and increasing electricity consumption further contribute to the challenges. Additionally, in treacherous mountainous areas where ethnic minorities reside, electricity remains scarce.

3. Unable to use renewable energy

Vietnam's lack of a proper electricity pricing mechanism hinders agreements between investors and EVN, preventing completed renewable energy projects such as solar and wind power from connecting to the grid. Thus, reliance on fossil fuels persists, unable to use renewable and clean energy.

4. Regional imbalance

There are a shortage of electricity in the Northern region while in the Middle and Southern region, there is a surplus of energy. This can result in the imbalance in economic and social development of Vietnam.

5. Difficulty improving quality of life

The lack of sufficient electricity supply will limit development and hinder businesses, schools, healthcare services, and basic utilities from functioning. This creates difficulties in improving the quality of life.

URGENT SOLUTIONS

Investing in renewable energy
Vietnam plans to implement solar projects on the rooftops of government offices and public institutions. Additionally, we should invest in wind energy by building more windmills on land and in the sea area.

Enhance awareness
Raise public awareness on electricity usage through education and campaigns promoting energy conservation. For example, turning off unused electrical devices and reducing unnecessary electricity consumption.

Establish reasonable electricity pricing mechanisms
The government can introduce regulations and transparent pricing systems to facilitate electricity trading between producers and power companies. Changes should be made considering production costs, efficiency, and environmental impacts.

Construct factories that meet the LOTUS standards
LOTUS (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design) is a green building rating system that promotes energy efficiency and sustainability, creating healthier environments and supporting sustainable development in Vietnam.

LONG-TERM SOLUTIONS

Investment
Investing in new product development and research involving innovative technology and its development can assist in providing new solutions for energy and electricity-related concerns.

Incentives and support policies
The government can motivate renewable energy investments through financial incentives, tax exemptions, input cost reductions, and favorable electricity purchase prices.

Planning and infrastructure development
Planning and development of infrastructure can be edited to support renewable energy. Predicting electricity demand, and collaborating with investors can improve sustainable projects.

Grid integration
The government can smoothly integrate renewable energy into the grid, improve infrastructure to support transition and development and create a smart grid to optimize power distribution and decrease electricity waste.

Enhancing international cooperation
Collaborating with international organizations, countries, and partners to share experiences and knowledge on renewable energy management and electricity pricing mechanisms could provide new insight into issues.

Enhancing energy management
Smart and efficient energy management may help companies and government organizations cut energy use and expenditures.

VIETNAM’S EFFORTS & FOREIGN COUNTRIES ASSISTANCE

Our country is taking several measures including investing in renewable energy sources, raising awareness and educating the public on energy-efficient electricity usage, and developing the electricity grid infrastructure to integrate renewable energy sources into the national system.

Vietnam is actively developing renewable energy projects, with 2022 seeing a total electricity production of 80,000 MW. It derived one-third of its energy from hydro, one-third from fossil fuels, and one-third from renewables. The country has established wind farms in regions like Binh Dinh, Quang Binh, Ninh Thuan (onshore), and Bac Lieu (offshore). Vietnam is also focused on meeting LOTUS standards by improving factories to reduce emissions and energy consumption during production. In the energy sector, Vietnam and South Korea have signed an MOU to collaborate on researching small-scale nuclear power technology.

Vietnam has secured substantial financial support from the EU, with a commitment of €22 billion ($26 billion USD) in 2019, representing one-third of the climate finance provided by developed countries. Germany, through the Just Energy Transition Partnership (JETP), plans to mobilize $15.5 billion over 3-5 years to aid Vietnam's green transformation. This funding might assist Vietnam in reaching its goal of achieving net-zero emissions by 2050, transitioning from fossil fuels to renewable energy, and fostering sustainable production and consumption models.
